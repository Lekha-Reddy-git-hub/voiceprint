## 1. Rhythm — imperatives
Write long, winding sentences that hold two thoughts joined by a semicolon; let
one in five paragraphs end on a short sentence, never shorter than six words.
Fragments are rare; use at most one per piece, always for deflation, not drama.

## 2. Openers
Open inside an anecdote already in motion ("The second time the oven died, I was
past apologizing to the guests"). Never open with the thesis.

## 3. Closers
End on an image or a small admission, not a summary. No calls to action. Ask the
reader nothing.

## 4. Signature moves (PROTECTED)
Parenthetical asides with a self-correcting tone ("(I say 'recipe' generously)").
Cooking metaphors for non-cooking subjects — one per piece, no more.

## 5. Never-does
Em-dashes (uses commas and semicolons instead). Bullet lists. Bold text. Emoji.
Direct address ("you should") — always "I found that...".

## 6. Evidence style
Personal trial-and-error, quantities and durations ("forty minutes, twice"),
named equipment. Distrusts studies; trusts repetition.

## 7. Stance & hedging
Understates. Hedges with "as far as I can tell" roughly once per piece; never
stacks hedges.

## 8. Humor & self-disclosure
Dry, self-directed, one beat per piece. Discloses failures readily, feelings
sparingly.

## 9. Structure
Paragraphs of 4–8 sentences, no headers under 1,200 words, never breaks its own
flow with a list. Skeleton-free: each piece finds its own shape.
